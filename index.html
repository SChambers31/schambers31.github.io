From Broadcast to Basket: What Ad Tech Needs to Learn from Local Commerce Platforms
I've been thinking a lot about where ad tech is heading, and I keep coming back to the same conclusion: the most interesting innovation isn't happening in traditional media. It's happening in your food delivery app.
Ad tech has spent years optimising for clicks. Marketplace platforms are optimising for something harder: actual transactions. That shift changes everything. Platforms like Amazon rewrote the model with closed-loop systems, first-party data, and direct attribution to purchase. Now the next evolution is happening within local commerce platforms like Just Eat and Uber Eats.
Ads as Discovery, Not Interruption
In a marketplace, the user isn't passively consuming content. They're actively deciding. The most effective ad products don't behave like traditional ads. They behave like ranking systems, recommendation engines, and discovery tools. A sponsored restaurant isn't just buying visibility. It's entering the consideration set at the exact moment of intent.
The Trade-Offs That Actually Matter
Designing ads in a marketplace means managing tension between three sides: too many paid placements and user trust erodes; too few, and revenue is left on the table. The highest bidder isn't always the best result for the user. The most effective platforms don't treat ads as an overlay. They integrate them into the core product logic.
What This Means for the Future of Ad Tech
The next wave of ad tech will be defined by first-party real-time intent signals, closed-loop measurement tied to outcomes, and ad formats that feel native to user decisions. In this world, the line between "ad" and "product feature" starts to disappear. The platforms that win won't be the ones with the most ads, but the ones where ads feel indistinguishable from value.

💬 Shelly's take
Scheduling in broadcast remains largely retrospective, we measure performance, adjust future grids, and optimise based on what has already happened. But audience behaviour is no longer cyclical or contained within reporting windows. I listened in on the OTTRED AI in Media Think Tank session on nowcasting, and one theme stood out: if audience discovery is happening in real time, optimisation logic may need to as well. The real constraint isn't the technology, it's structural readiness. Fragmented data infrastructure, inconsistent metadata, and organisational trust in AI-led decision support. AI will not resolve those gaps, it will expose them. Which makes nowcasting less a modelling challenge and more a maturity test. I'm particularly interested in how this could evolve within FAST environments and promotional strategy, areas where experimentation feels both commercially relevant and operationally feasible.
